What is the Air Quality Index?

The Air Quality Index (AQI) is the system used to warn the public when air pollution is dangerous by:

Tracking Smog
Monitoring Air Particles
Indicating Risk Levels

The Air Quality Index Table (AQI)

Name Index Value Advisory
Good 0 to 50 None
Moderate 51 to 100 Usually sensitive individuals should consider limiting prolonged outdoor exertion.
Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups 101 to 150 Children, active adults, and people with respiratory disease, such as asthma, should limit prolonged outdoor exertion.
Unhealthy 151 to 200 Children, active adults, and people with respiratory disease, such as asthma, should avoid outdoor exertion; everyone else should limit prolonged outdoor exertion.
Very Unhealthy 201 to 300 Children, active adults, and people with respiratory disease, such as asthma, should avoid outdoor exertion; everyone else should limit outdoor exertion.
Hazardous 301 to 500 Everyone should avoid all physical activity outdoors.

How does it work?

Daily measurements quantify air pollution levels, utilizing a scale from 0, indicating pristine air, to 500, signifying an imminent threat to public health. The Air Quality Index (AQI) categorizes pollution into six levels, each assigned a name, a corresponding color, and advisory recommendations. AQI values equal to or below 100 are deemed acceptable for nearly everyone. However, when surpassing 100, air quality is classified as unhealthy, with heightened values indicating an elevated risk of health issues for a larger population.

How can you Ensure Your Family's Safety?

  • Don't presume safety solely based on good health; air pollution can jeopardize anyone's well-being. Pay attention to how you feel on days with high pollution and take measures to safeguard yourself.

  • Identify if you or a family member is at a higher risk from air pollution. Children, young adults, pregnant individuals, those with chronic illnesses, and those who spend considerable time outdoors face elevated risks. They should take extra precautions against the effects of ozone and particle pollution.

  • Adjust your plans on days with a forecast of code orange or worse air quality. Minimize outdoor activities, especially in high-traffic areas. Prolonged exposure exacerbates health effects, and strenuous exercise intensifies the impact. Stay indoors on particularly poor air quality days.

  • Advocate for clean air. Express your concerns to local authorities regarding the health impact of air pollution and endorse more robust pollution control measures.

MetroBreathe

MetroBreathe is a smart air pollution monitoring system and smog prediction model developed for Metro Manila. It aims to provide real-time air quality data and predict smog occurrences, aiding local authorities in effective air quality management and promoting public health and environmental sustainability.
